# Step B — Academic Research Question

**Derived from:** The short story of Sofia, Arancio the clownfish, the abandoned masseria, and the wild grapevine that sustained life in forgotten terrain.

---

## Primary Research Question

> **To what extent do abandoned agricultural structures in Southern Italy function as unintentional ecological and viticultural refugia, and how can the adaptive biological resilience observed in post-abandonment landscapes inform precision conservation and sustainable land-revitalisation strategies for small-scale Mediterranean viticulture?**

---

## Sub-questions

1. What microclimatic and hydrological conditions within derelict rural buildings (masserie, terraced vineyards, cisterns) enable unmanaged grapevines (*Vitis vinifera*) to survive and self-organise beyond conventional cultivation parameters?

2. In what ways do the phenological responses of feral grapevine cultivars in abandoned Southern Italian landscapes differ from managed counterparts, and what do those differences reveal about climate resilience?

3. How might remote-sensing, IoT-based soil monitoring, and machine-learning models trained on Mediterranean polyculture data be deployed to detect and preserve these accidental refugia before small producers lose them to climate stress or urban encroachment?

4. What institutional, regulatory, and market barriers prevent micro-scale Italian wine producers (< 5 ha) from adopting precision agriculture tools already proven at industrial scale?

---

## Theoretical Framework

The question sits at the intersection of **landscape ecology**, **abandonment geography** (Plieninger & Bieling, 2012), and **precision agriculture technology adoption** — specifically examining whether lessons encoded in resilient feral viticulture can be translated back into actionable, affordable intelligence for the small producers who are most exposed to climate volatility and least equipped to respond to it.
